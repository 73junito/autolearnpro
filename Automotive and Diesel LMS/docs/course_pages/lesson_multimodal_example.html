üîí Safety Precautions

  • Wear safety glasses when working with brake fluid
  • Brake fluid is corrosive - avoid contact with paint and skin
  • Ensure vehicle is properly supported on jack stands

üîß Required Tools & Materials

  • Brake fluid (DOT 3 or DOT 4 as specified)
  • Brake bleeder wrench or box wrench (typically 10mm)
  • Clear plastic tubing (1/4" diameter)
  • Catch bottle for old fluid
  • Jack and jack stands
  • Torque wrench

üìã Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Check fluid level in master cylinder reservoir
    Remove reservoir cap and verify fluid is between MIN and MAX marks. Low fluid indicates worn pads or possible leak.
  2. Inspect brake lines for damage or leaks
    Look for corrosion, kinks, or wet spots along steel brake lines. Pay special attention to connections at master cylinder and calipers.
    üí° Tip: Use a flashlight to check lines near the fuel tank where road salt often causes corrosion.
  3. Test pedal feel and travel
    With engine running, press brake pedal firmly. Pedal should feel solid and stop 1-2 inches from floor. Spongy feel indicates air in system.
  4. Bleed brake system if necessary
    Start with wheel furthest from master cylinder (typically right rear). Attach tubing to bleeder valve and submerge other end in catch bottle.
  5. Verify repair and test drive
    Road test in safe area. Check for proper pedal feel, straight stops, and no pulling to one side.

‚ùå Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Don't let master cylinder run dry during bleeding
  • Don't mix DOT 3 and DOT 5 brake fluids
  • Don't reuse old brake fluid

‚úÖ Verification & Testing

Final verification should include: firm pedal feel, no leaks at connections, proper fluid level, and successful road test with straight stops at various speeds.

Think of it like squeezing a water balloon. When you squeeze one side, the whole balloon feels the pressure, not just where you're squeezing. That's exactly how your brake system works! REFERENCE DIAGRAM 2

Now, let's look at the master cylinder in detail. Modern vehicles use a dual-circuit design with primary and secondary pistons. This is a critical safety feature. If one circuit fails, you still have braking on at least two wheels. Pretty smart engineering! PAUSE

One common issue students ask about is spongy brake pedal feel. This almost always indicates air in the hydraulic system. Remember - air is compressible, but brake fluid isn't. When air gets in, you're compressing the air instead of transmitting force to the brakes. That's why proper bleeding procedure is so important.
